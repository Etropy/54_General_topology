Proposition.

Every compact metric space is second countable.

Proof.

Let (X,d) be a compact metric space, and for each n∈ℤ+ define 𝒜n={B⁢(x,1/n):x∈X}, where B⁢(x,1/n) denotes the open ball centered about x of http://planetmath.org/node/1296radius 1/n. Each such collection is an open cover of the compact space X, so for each n∈ℤ+ there exists a finite collection ℬn⊆𝒜n that X. Put ℬ=⋃n=1∞ℬn. Being a countable union of finite sets, it follows that ℬ is countable; we assert that it forms a basis for the metric topology on X. The first property of a basis is satisfied trivially, as each set ℬn is an open cover of X. For the second property, let x,x1,x2∈X, n1,n2∈ℤ+, and suppose x∈B⁢(x1,1/n1)∩B⁢(x2,1/n2). Because the sets B⁢(x1,1/n1) and B⁢(x2,1/n2) are open in the metric topology on X, their intersection is also open, so there exists ϵ>0 such that B⁢(x,ϵ)⊆B⁢(x1,1/n1)∩B⁢(x2,1/n2). Select N∈ℤ+ such that 1/N<ϵ. There must exist x3∈X such that x∈B⁢(x3,1/2⁢N) (since ℬ2⁢N is an open cover of X). To see that B⁢(x3,1/2⁢N)⊆B⁢(x1,1/n1)∩B⁢(x2,1/n2), let y∈B⁢(x3,1/2⁢N). Then we have

d⁢(x,y)≤d⁢(x,x3)+d⁢(x3,y)<12⁢N+12⁢N=1N<ϵ⁢, (1)

so that y∈B⁢(x,ϵ), from which it follows that y∈B⁢(x1,1/n1)∩B⁢(x2,1/n2), hence that B⁢(x3,1/2⁢N)⊆B⁢(x1,1/n1)∩B⁢(x2,1/n2). Thus the countable collection ℬ forms a basis for a topology on X; the verification that the topology by ℬ is in fact the metric topology follows by an to that used to verify the second property of a basis, and completes the proof that X is second countable. ∎

It is worth nothing that, because a countable union of countable sets is countable, it would have been sufficient to assume that (X,d) was a Lindelöf space.
